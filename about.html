Supreme Court Analytics & Visualizations


About the Project

We made these visualizations to let people explore and research the Supreme Court. Legal research has always been largely about reading opinions and other legal documents, and this will continue to be the case in the future. However, through the use of tools like d3.js, we hope to map data from the Court to visualizations that provide a starting point for legal researchers.

Data from the Supreme Court is generally difficult to work with. Since 1991, the Supreme Court has published Bound Volumes in PDF form for each term. In 1997-1998, docket numbers became more common to cite cases, as official U.S.S.C. citations were not available until the bound volumes were published.

Harold J. Spaeth started the Supreme Court Database in the 1980s at Washington University in St. Louis, and it has been available online for the past decade. It breaks down cases into useful variables beginning with the 1946 term. They are currently in the process of applying for funding to extend the database back to 1792.
